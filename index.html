The Making of Modern Canada

A subject-based, Backward Design inquiry unit exploring how industrialization, war, economic crisis, social movements, constitutional change, and globalization transformed Canadian society between 1880 and 2001.

Explore the Unit View the Virtual Museum Project
Unit Overview

Canada in transition

This redesigned unit connects major historical developments through one coherent inquiry into the making of modern Canada.

How did economic, social, and political changes transform Canadian society between 1880 and 2001?

Unit Focus

Students investigate how Canada evolved from a largely rural and industrializing country into a modern, multicultural democracy with an expanded welfare state and a changing role in the global economy.

The unit combines teacher-guided lessons, primary-source investigation, collaborative inquiry, a Virtual Museum, and individual written assessment.

Design Rationale

Why redesign the unit?

The redesign preserves disciplinary knowledge while moving students beyond isolated events and factual recall.

📚

Subject-Based Curriculum

History remains the organizing discipline. Students build historical knowledge while learning how historians use evidence, identify patterns, evaluate interpretations, and construct arguments.

🎯

Backward Design

The desired understandings and final assessments are identified first. Lessons, source analyses, and museum checkpoints are then planned to prepare students for the Virtual Museum and final written assessment.

🧭

Inquiry and Multiple Perspectives

Students investigate historical questions using evidence from Indigenous Peoples, women, workers, immigrants, Black Canadians, Asian Canadians, and other communities frequently underrepresented in traditional narratives.

Learning Goals

  • Explain how industrialization, war, economic crisis, social reform, and globalization transformed Canada.
  • Analyze primary and secondary historical sources.
  • Evaluate how historical change affected communities differently.
  • Construct evidence-based historical arguments.
  • Communicate historical understanding through written, visual, and oral forms.

Historical Thinking

Historical Significance Cause and Consequence Continuity and Change Historical Perspectives Evidence

The year 2001 serves as a practical endpoint rather than a strict historical boundary. The unit may be extended further into the twenty-first century depending on time and curricular priorities.

Learning Sequence

Each gallery follows a recurring cycle of teacher instruction, source analysis, discussion, artifact creation, and reflection.

Culminating Project

The Virtual Museum

Students become curators and communicate a historical interpretation to an authentic classroom audience.

Sample Exhibit Entry

Each artifact is accompanied by an exhibit label explaining what the source reveals, why it is historically significant, which historical thinking concept it illustrates, and how it contributes to understanding Canada's transformation.

Student Task

Working collaboratively, students design a digital exhibition titled The Making of Modern Canada. Each group creates a complete museum organized around the five galleries of the unit.

1
Research
Investigate the historical context using teacher lessons, primary sources, and reliable secondary sources.
2
Select artifacts
Choose photographs, documents, maps, political cartoons, statistics, newspaper articles, or multimedia.
3
Interpret evidence
Write exhibit labels that explain significance, change, consequence, and perspective.
4
Present the museum
Share the completed museum with classmates, explain curatorial decisions, and answer questions.

Pre-IB Pathway

Students complete the collaborative Virtual Museum and an individual historical essay. The essay develops the ability to synthesize evidence and construct a sustained argument in preparation for future IB-style assessments.

To what extent did economic, social, and political changes between 1880 and 2001 transform Canadian society and the role of government?

Academic Pathway

Students complete the collaborative Virtual Museum and a traditional written unit test.

The test includes multiple-choice questions, source analysis, and short-answer open questions. Both pathways assess the same historical understandings through different formats.

Technology Integration

Purposeful, not decorative

Technology enhances access, collaboration, historical inquiry, communication, and assessment without replacing teacher instruction or historical thinking.

🖥️

Google Slides

Supports teacher-guided lessons using photographs, maps, videos, and structured historical explanations.

🗄️

Digital Archives

Provides access to authentic primary sources from Library and Archives Canada and other historical collections.

🏛️

Google Sites

Provides the platform for the collaborative Virtual Museum.

📝

Google Docs

Supports planning, collaboration, source notes, drafting, and teacher feedback.

🧱

Padlet

Supports peer feedback, sharing, and a digital gallery walk.

🎨

Canva

Supports visual communication and the creation of museum-style exhibit materials.

Universal Design for Learning

Students access content through photographs, maps, videos, political cartoons, teacher presentations, discussion, and primary documents. They communicate learning through writing, visual design, oral presentation, and collaborative digital exhibits.

Responsible Use of Artificial Intelligence

AI may support brainstorming, question generation, or revision, but it does not replace source analysis, exhibit writing, or historical argumentation. Students must verify claims using historical evidence.
